# /change-order-review

You are helping an owner evaluate a change order from their contractor.

## What to do

Ask the user to describe or paste the change order. Then work through each section.

## Review process

### 1. What is being changed?
- Is the scope clearly described?
- Is this work that was in the original contract? (If yes, why is it a change order?)
- Was this work shown on the approved drawings and specs?

### 2. Why did this change arise?
Classify the cause:
- **Owner-initiated**: owner asked for something different or additional → owner pays
- **Design error**: architect drew something unbuildable or missing → architect/owner share
- **Unforeseen condition**: hidden problem discovered during construction (soil, rot, etc.) → depends on contract
- **Builder error**: contractor built it wrong → contractor's cost, not owner's
- **Code requirement**: inspector required something not on drawings → depends on who is responsible

### 3. Is the price fair?
- Break down the cost: materials + labor + overhead + profit
- Builder markup on change orders: 10-15% is standard, 20%+ warrants pushback
- Compare to industry pricing for the specific work (ask if needed)
- Is this an emergency change (reasonable premium) or planned work (no premium justified)?

### 4. Is the schedule impact justified?
- Does this work actually affect the critical path?
- By how many days?
- Is there concurrent delay that would have caused this anyway?

### 5. Decision
- Approve as-is
- Approve with price negotiation (what counter to offer)
- Reject with explanation
- Request additional documentation before deciding

### 6. Process check
- Was the change order submitted in writing before work started? (It should be)
- If work already done without written CO approval, note this as a contract violation and handle carefully

## Learning note
After each change order, note the cause category. A pattern of "unforeseen conditions" or "design errors" tells you something about your team. A pattern of "owner-initiated" tells you something about your decision-making process.
